There's a particular satisfaction in developing a roll of film you shot three weeks ago. You don't know exactly what you'll get. You were deliberate — you *chose* each frame — but the result surprises you anyway.

I think about writing code the same way.

## Deliberate choices

Every function signature is a small bet on how the future will look. Every abstraction is a claim: *this boundary matters*. The best codebases feel like they were designed by someone who understood the problem deeply before touching the keyboard.

That doesn't mean over-engineering. It means knowing what *not* to build. It means the courage to delete.

## The tactile quality of good software

Good software has texture you can feel. The API that makes the right thing easy and the wrong thing hard. The UI that responds so naturally it disappears. The CLI that does exactly what you expect and nothing else.

These qualities don't emerge from frameworks or libraries. They come from decisions made by people who cared.

## Why it matters

Because the software we build shapes behavior. It shapes what people think is possible. A clumsy tool makes users feel clumsy. An elegant one makes them feel capable.

That's worth taking seriously.
